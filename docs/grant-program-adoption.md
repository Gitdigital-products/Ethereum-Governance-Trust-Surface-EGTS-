📘 EGTS Adoption Path for Grant Programs
Version: 0.2.x  
Status: Active Draft  
Audience: Ethereum Foundation ESP reviewers, public‑good grant programs, ecosystem funding bodies  
Purpose: Explain how EGTS strengthens governance, auditability, and contributor legitimacy for grant‑funded ecosystems  

---

1. Why Grant Programs Need EGTS

Grant programs operate across diverse ecosystems, contributors, and governance structures.  
They face recurring challenges:

1.1 Legitimacy Ambiguity
- Who approved this milestone?  
- Who executed this deliverable?  
- Who validated this contributor’s authority?  

1.2 Fragmented Governance Metadata
Every DAO, L2, and protocol uses different formats for:

- proposals  
- approvals  
- milestones  
- contributor roles  

1.3 Missing or Incomplete Audit Trails
Grant reviewers often cannot verify:

- proposal lineage  
- validator authority  
- execution legitimacy  
- contributor identity  

1.4 Cross‑Ecosystem Blind Spots
A contributor may be legitimate in one ecosystem but unknown in another.

---

2. How EGTS Solves These Problems

EGTS provides a universal trust‑surface standard that grant programs can rely on.

2.1 Standardized Governance Events
Every proposal, approval, milestone, and execution is represented in a consistent schema.

2.2 Verifiable Contributor Legitimacy
Roles and authority are validated using EGTS rules.

2.3 Portable Trust Signals
Grant programs can trust governance actions across:

- DAOs  
- L2 ecosystems  
- public‑good systems  
- protocol governance  

2.4 Complete Audit Trails
Every validated governance action is recorded in an append‑only audit log.

2.5 Chain‑Agnostic Interoperability
EGTS works across:

- Ethereum L1  
- L2s  
- app‑chains  
- off‑chain governance systems  

---

3. Adoption Levels for Grant Programs

Grant programs can adopt EGTS at three levels.

---

Level 1 — Governance Event Intake (Minimum Viable Adoption)

Grant programs require applicants to submit:

- EGTS‑formatted governance events  
- proposal metadata  
- contributor identity  
- authority references  

Outcome:  
Grant reviewers receive structured, verifiable governance data.

---

Level 2 — Trust‑Surface Verification (Recommended)

Grant programs require:

- validated trust signals  
- validator identities  
- authority scope checks  
- integrity hashes  

Outcome:  
Grant reviewers can verify legitimacy without relying on screenshots, links, or unverifiable claims.

---

Level 3 — Full Audit Log Integration (Ideal)

Grant programs require:

- audit log entries for all governance actions  
- hash lineage  
- validator signatures  
- audit period metadata  

Outcome:  
Grant reviewers gain complete, portable governance auditability.

---

4. Benefits for Grant Programs

4.1 Reduced Governance Risk
EGTS ensures:

- proposals are legitimate  
- approvals are valid  
- milestones are real  
- contributors have authority  

4.2 Increased Transparency
Audit logs provide:

- event lineage  
- validator signatures  
- integrity proofs  

4.3 Cross‑Ecosystem Trust
Grant programs can trust governance actions across multiple ecosystems.

4.4 Lower Administrative Overhead
EGTS standardizes:

- proposal formats  
- milestone verification  
- contributor legitimacy  
- audit trails  

4.5 Better Funding Outcomes
Grant programs can:

- detect governance inconsistencies  
- verify contributor authority  
- ensure milestone authenticity  
- reduce fraud and misrepresentation  

---

5. How Grant Programs Can Require EGTS

Grant programs can adopt EGTS by adding a simple requirement:

> “All governance actions related to this grant must be submitted in EGTS‑compatible format.”

This includes:

- proposal creation  
- approvals  
- milestone verification  
- execution events  

Grant programs may also require:

- trust signals for each governance action  
- audit log entries for each milestone  

---

6. Example Grant Workflow Using EGTS

Step 1 — Proposal Submission
Applicant submits:

- EGTS governance event  
- contributor identity  
- authority reference  

Step 2 — Approval Verification
Grant reviewers verify:

- trust signal  
- validator identities  
- authority scope  

Step 3 — Milestone Completion
Applicant submits:

- milestone execution event  
- validation trust signal  
- audit log entry  

Step 4 — Final Audit
Grant reviewers verify:

- event lineage  
- hash integrity  
- validator signatures
